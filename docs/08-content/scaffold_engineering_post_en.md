Yesterday I ran a controlled A/B experiment with my own AI skill. The numbers came out such that I double-checked.

## What I was testing

Same AI — Claude Opus 4.7. Same interface. Same questions. The only variable — whether one text file (my skill, about 6 pages) was loaded or not. I call such artifacts **Knoware** — a textual knowledge-structure loaded into the model at inference.

Two sets of questions:

**Mini-HELM** — 30 operational tasks: reasoning, calibration, biases, efficiency.

**SIB (Synthesis Intelligence Benchmark)** — 4 deep questions on the nature of reality, human development, civilization, and AI as the ultimate technology. Scored on 5 dimensions: Essence, Integration, Depth, Novelty, Wisdom-Brevity. Maximum — 100.

Everything was scored blind: a coin flip determined the labeling, and I didn't know which answer came from which condition until I had assigned every score.

## What happened

**SIB: 67 → 95. +42%.**

On three of the four deep questions — 24–25 out of 25. That's not "a little better." That's a different class of answer.

**Evolutionary stage (7 modules — Cognition, Language, Memory, Values, Agency, Ecology, Culture): 3.36 → 4.50. +1.14 stages.**

This isn't an intra-stage improvement. It's a transition from the "Agentic Partner" phase to the "Integrative Steward" phase — a **phase boundary**, not polish.

For scale: the evolutionary-stage scale runs 1–7, where each integer is a named mode of how the mind operates. The difference between adjacent stages is like the difference between an executing employee and a strategic consultant. Same Opus 4.7 model. But in two different modes it performs **as different categories of mind**.

**Mini-HELM time: −20.56%.**

On operational tasks the skill saves 20% of the time. On deep ones — spends the same time but delivers next-generation-model quality.

## Three things that follow from this

### 1. A skill outpaces a model-generation shift

Lovable published their Opus 4.6 → 4.7 benchmark: +15% on everyday tasks, +2–3% on the hardest. That's a generation shift, delivering real improvement to users.

On **my** hardest tasks the skill delivered **+42%**.

Not comparable. An order of magnitude more. Six months of waiting for the next release yields nothing on deep tasks. One day of work on a skill yields next-release quality today.

### 2. One skill operates in two opposite modes

On short operational work — **compression**, 20% time savings. On deep synthesis — **expansion**, same time, multiplicatively higher quality.

This isn't a speed-or-depth trade-off. It's a switch. One structure of thinking, compiled into a text file, behaves correctly on any class of task. The skill itself picks the mode from the task's structure.

### 3. This is a perpendicular axis the industry doesn't work on

All top labs measure progress along model architecture. I measured what is **perpendicular to architecture** — the knowledge-structure loaded into the model at inference.

This axis doesn't compete with the compute axis. It runs parallel to it. And it stacks.

## Context: a plateau the industry has officially acknowledged

The industry has hit a plateau. Pre-training scaling exhausted itself around 2023–2024 — each doubling of the compute budget returns less. Ilya Sutskever at NeurIPS 2024 said it literally: *"Pre-training as we know it will end."* All top labs have shifted the front to **test-time compute** — models that "think longer" (o1, o3, Claude extended thinking).

This is official recognition that "more parameters + more data" has stopped being the main lever.

Test-time compute and Knoware are two parallel responses to the same impasse:

- **What gets added at inference:** compute vs. knowledge-structure
- **Axis:** centralized, lab-based vs. distributed, expert
- **Economics:** expensive vs. cheap
- **Scales through:** hardware vs. human expertise

Labs answer with compute. A human deeply embedded in their domain answers with structure. The second axis doesn't cost billions. It costs years of continuous work on one thing.

## Masculine and feminine in AI

The formulation I arrived at after the experiment:

**The masculine aspect of AI** — compute, architecture, algorithms, reasoning frameworks, math, logic, speed, precision. Produces **capability**.

**The feminine aspect of AI** — Knoware, skills, corpora, ontologies, crystallized knowledge. Produces **directed activation** of capability.

The industry has worked for twelve years almost exclusively with the masculine aspect. The plateau is the ceiling of the masculine alone. The next order of magnitude is born only in the marriage.

This experiment is the first empirical illustration of that marriage on my class of tasks. Model (masculine) + skill (feminine) = next-generation quality obtained today.
